SECTION: Understanding Oil & Gas Equipment Categories (400 words)

Cover the full production lifecycle:
- Upstream: drilling, wellhead, artificial lift, surface production equipment
- Midstream: compression, processing, pipeline equipment
- Downstream: refinery, terminals, distribution (mostly industrial, covered separately)
- Oilfield services: workover, well stimulation, wireline

Why oil and gas equipment financing is different from general equipment:
- Commodity price risk: lenders worry that if oil drops to $40/bbl, borrower's income evaporates
- Specialized collateral: a rod pump in a West Texas oilfield is hard to repo and sell
- Energy-sector lenders (First National Capital, Frost Bank, Pioneer Natural Resources' banking partners) understand the business better than general lenders
- Equipment often located on leased mineral rights — collateral recovery is complicated
- Working interest financing vs equipment financing — many operators use their working interest as collateral instead

- EPA SPCC Plan (Spill Prevention, Control, Countermeasure) — required for facilities over 1,320 gallons above-ground storage

- OQ (Operator Qualification) per DOT 49 CFR 192/195 for pipeline work
- Blasting/explosives — ATF license for perforation charges

Write 600 words covering:

Business model options:
1. Independent owner-operator (most freedom, most risk, highest potential)
2. Branded dealer (Shell, BP, ExxonMobil, Chevron — supply agreement, brand support, some volume guarantees)
3. Commission agent (you work for the oil company — they own equipment)
4. DODO (Dealer Owned, Dealer Operated) — you own the site, choose your own supplier

Total startup cost breakdown:
Land/building: $200,000–$2,000,000+ depending on market
Underground storage tanks and installation: $80,000–$250,000
Fuel dispensers (8-12 typical): $40,000–$150,000
Canopy: $45,000–$150,000 installed
Site work (paving, drainage, lighting): $50,000–$200,000
C-store buildout (if included): $100,000–$400,000
POS and IT systems: $15,000–$45,000
Permits, licenses, environmental: $10,000–$30,000
Initial fuel inventory: $50,000–$150,000
Working capital: $50,000–$100,000
Total range: $640,000–$3,475,000+

Financing approach:
- SBA 504 (most popular): covers real estate and equipment, 10-20% down, long terms — ideal for gas station
- SBA 7(a): more flexible, higher rates, good for working capital component
- Commercial real estate loan for the land/building portion
- Equipment financing for dispensers, tanks, canopy separately
- Environmental lenders: banks in petroleum-heavy regions (Texas, Oklahoma, Louisiana) understand gas station financing better

Licensing requirements:
- EPA UST operator training (Class A, B, C operators required by EPA)
- State petroleum storage tank permit
- Fire department inspection and permit (tanks and dispensers are fire hazards)
- Business license
- Sales tax permit for fuel
- State fuel distributor/retailer license
- Local zoning approval (gas stations often require conditional use permit)
- Food service permit (if selling prepared food)
- Alcohol license (if selling beer/wine)
- Weights and measures certification (dispensers must be certified accurate by state)

Income potential:
- Fuel margin: $0.15–$0.35 per gallon (highly variable, diesel often higher)
- Average independent station sells 80,000–150,000 gallons/month
- Fuel income: $12,000–$52,500/month gross
- C-store: $30,000–$150,000/month revenue depending on volume and services
- Car wash (if attached): $8,000–$50,000/month additional

Business model choice:
1. Self-serve only: $150,000–$500,000 startup, least staff, passive-ish income, declining market
2. In-bay automatic only: $300,000–$700,000, 1-2 staff, steady traffic
3. Express exterior tunnel: $800,000–$2,000,000, 5-10 staff, monthly membership model, most popular new model
4. Full-service tunnel: $1,500,000–$4,000,000+, 20-40 staff, high revenue potential, labor intensive

The monthly membership model revolution:
The Unlimited Wash Club (UWC) model charges $20–$40/month for unlimited washes. This converts per-car revenue to recurring monthly revenue. Key metrics: if 500 members at $30/month = $15,000/month recurring before any additional customers. Lenders love this model because it's more predictable than per-car revenue. Sonny's, DRB Systems, and Unitec all have software to manage membership programs.

Revenue model for express exterior tunnel (150 cars/day average):
- Pay-per-wash: 150 cars × $9 average = $1,350/day × 365 = $492,750/year
- Monthly membership (target 600 members): 600 × $30 = $18,000/month = $216,000/year
- Total blended: $492,750 + $216,000 = $708,750/year gross
- Industry average for express exterior: $700,000–$1,500,000/year revenue
- Net margin: 25–40% for well-run express exterior = $175,000–$600,000 net

- Business license

- Building permit for tunnel structure
