Overview

Agricultural Equipment Financing: What Sets It Apart

Agricultural equipment financing differs from other commercial equipment lending in several important ways. Farm income is inherently seasonal — most revenue arrives at harvest, creating cash flow patterns that standard monthly payment schedules don't accommodate well. OEM manufacturers (John Deere, CNH Industrial, AGCO) recognized this decades ago and built seasonal payment programs, annual payment options, and 0% promotional financing into their dealer finance arms specifically for farmers.

Farm Credit Services and AgDirect are the other major players in agricultural finance. Farm Credit is a government-sponsored enterprise (GSE) specifically chartered to serve agriculture, and its cooperative structure enables member farmers to access below-market rates and specialized agricultural lending expertise. AgDirect is Farm Credit's equipment-specific lending arm, competing directly with dealer finance programs on tractors, combines, and planters.

The collateral picture for agricultural equipment is generally favorable. Major-brand farm equipment — John Deere and Case IH especially — holds value extremely well. A well-maintained John Deere 8R tractor or S780 combine typically retains 50–65% of its original value after 5 years. This strong collateral position gives lenders confidence, and it's one reason agricultural equipment financing is accessible even for farmers with below-average credit scores when their equipment and income history are strong.

Licensing & Compliance

Agricultural Business Licensing Requirements

USDA Farm Number

Farm Service Agency (FSA) farm number required to participate in USDA programs, commodity loans, and crop insurance. Free to register at your local FSA office.

Pesticide Applicator License

Commercial pesticide application requires state pesticide applicator license. Private applicator (own crops) has lower requirements. EPA regulates under FIFRA; states administer licensing.

CDL for Grain Hauling

Class A CDL required to haul grain semi-trailers over 26,001 lbs GVWR. Agricultural exemptions exist in many states for vehicles operating within 150 miles of the farm.

Organic Certification

USDA National Organic Program (NOP) certification required to sell products as "organic." Certification through USDA-accredited certifiers. 3-year transition period before first organic harvest.

FSMA Produce Safety Rule

FDA Food Safety Modernization Act rules apply to farms with over $25,000 in produce sales. Water testing, hygiene, and recordkeeping requirements. Phased implementation by farm size.

Anhydrous Ammonia License

Many states require anhydrous ammonia handling certification. OSHA Process Safety Management may apply to large operations. Specialized training required for safe handling.

Common Questions

Agricultural Equipment Financing — FAQ

What are the monthly payments on a new John Deere combine?
A new John Deere S780 combine at $525,000 financed over 84 months at 6.5% APR runs approximately $7,928/month. The flagship X9 1100 at $650,000 over 84 months runs approximately $9,808/month. equipment lenders frequently offers 0% or low-rate promotional financing at planting and harvest seasons for current-model combines, which can significantly reduce these figures for qualified buyers with strong credit and established farming history.
Can I get seasonal payment structures for farm equipment?
Yes. equipment lenders, equipment lenders, and AGCO Finance all offer seasonal payment programs. Common structures include: skip payments during planting season (March–May), balloon payments aligned to fall harvest (October–December), annual payment programs for one large payment per year, or deferred first payment for 6-12 months. Farm Credit / AgDirect also offers seasonal structures for large purchases. These programs recognize that farm income is concentrated around harvest rather than distributed monthly.
What credit score is needed for agricultural equipment financing?
OEM programs (equipment lenders, equipment lenders) typically work best with 650+ credit scores for competitive rates. Farm Credit and AgDirect are often more flexible for established farmers, focusing on farm income history and assets rather than strictly on credit scores. Alternative agricultural lenders work with scores as low as 580-600, especially when the farm has proven income history in bank statements. The USDA FSA Beginning Farmer loan program specifically accommodates borrowers who can't get conventional credit.
Is it better to finance through equipment lenders or a bank?
equipment lenders and OEM programs frequently offer promotional rates — 0% APR for 12-24 months or special fixed rates on current-model equipment — that commercial banks cannot match at point of sale. For large purchases, it's worth getting quotes from the dealer program, Farm Credit/AgDirect, and a local agricultural bank. OEM promotional rates often win for qualified buyers, but Farm Credit's long-term rates may be better for non-promotional periods. Always compare the total cost of the loan, not just the payment.
Can a new farm operation finance equipment?
New farming operations can access several pathways: (1) USDA FSA Beginning Farmer Direct Loans offer below-market rates with flexible underwriting specifically for new farmers; (2) Equipment-specific lenders evaluate the machinery collateral heavily; (3) Dealer programs with 20-30% down may work for startup situations. Most lenders want at least one year of farm income history, though strong personal financial strength (high net worth, stable off-farm income) can substitute in some programs.
Does Section 179 apply to farm equipment?
Yes — tractors, combines, planters, sprayers, grain handling equipment, and most other farm machinery qualifies for Section 179 expensing up to $1,220,000 in 2024. Grain bins and irrigation systems may qualify as single-purpose agricultural structures under separate depreciation rules. Farmers also benefit from 150% declining balance depreciation on 5-year agricultural property. Consult your farm tax advisor or CPA for specifics — farm taxation has many specialized rules.
What is the difference between ag equipment financing and an FSA loan?
FSA (USDA Farm Service Agency) loans are government programs with subsidized below-market rates, designed for beginning farmers, farmers denied conventional credit, and emergency situations. FSA Guaranteed Loans are commercial loans with government backing. Standard agricultural equipment financing from dealers, banks, and Farm Credit is commercial without government involvement. FSA loans require more documentation and eligibility verification but offer the lowest rates available. Many farms use commercial financing for routine equipment and FSA programs for larger capital investments or startup situations.
How does financing a $600,000 combine harvester work?
Financing a $600,000 combine requires strong documentation: 680+ credit score (or strong farm financials), 2-3 years of farm tax returns showing revenue, 10-20% down ($60,000-$120,000), and 60-96 month loan term. equipment lenders, equipment lenders, and AgDirect are the primary lenders. Seasonal payment structures are commonly available — many farmers make one annual payment after harvest rather than 12 monthly payments. The combine serves as primary collateral; major brand combines retain 40-60% of value after 5-7 years, which supports strong lending terms.
