Common Questions

Commercial Equipment Financing — FAQ

What types of commercial equipment can be financed?
Virtually any type of commercial or business equipment can be financed, including construction machinery (excavators, bulldozers, cranes), agricultural equipment (tractors, combines, planters), CNC and manufacturing equipment, medical and dental equipment (MRI machines, CT scanners, dental chairs), forklifts and warehouse equipment, HVAC and mechanical systems, forestry and tree service equipment, restaurant and food service equipment, fitness equipment, laundry equipment, car wash systems, printing and signage equipment, oil and gas equipment, mining equipment, and data center infrastructure. As long as the equipment is used primarily for business purposes and has a quantifiable value, it can generally be financed. The key variables that affect financing terms are the equipment's resale value, the borrower's credit profile, and the lender's familiarity with that equipment category.
How does equipment financing work for very large purchases over $1 million?
Equipment purchases over $1 million are typically handled by equipment manufacturers' captive finance arms (equipment lenders, equipment lenders, equipment lenders), large bank equipment finance divisions (Wells Fargo Equipment Finance, US Bank), or specialized large-ticket equipment finance companies. These transactions require formal financial statements (audited or reviewed), 2-3 years of business tax returns, detailed equipment appraisals or dealer quotes, and documentation of the business's debt service capacity. Terms can extend to 84-120 months for very large purchases. The Section 179 deduction is capped at $1,220,000 in 2024, so purchases above this amount require bonus depreciation for additional first-year tax deductions. Working with an equipment financing broker who specializes in large-ticket transactions is often valuable for purchases above $500,000.
What is the minimum equipment purchase amount that lenders will finance?
Most equipment lenders have a practical minimum of $5,000-$10,000 per transaction. Some specialty lenders work with amounts as low as $2,500, but micro-ticket transactions often carry higher interest rates to offset fixed underwriting costs. For equipment purchases under $25,000, alternative lenders and online platforms typically offer the fastest and most accessible programs. Some business credit card programs allow smaller equipment purchases with deferred interest periods. For most commercial equipment that businesses typically finance — excavators, tractors, CNC machines, forklifts — amounts are well above typical lender minimums, generally starting at $25,000-$50,000 for useful commercial-grade equipment.
Can I finance multiple pieces of equipment in one application?
Yes. Many equipment lenders offer blanket financing or equipment line-of-credit programs that allow you to finance multiple pieces of equipment under a single approval. This is particularly useful for businesses building out a fleet or equipping a new facility. OEM dealers often offer package deals with combined financing across multiple machines from the same brand. For mixed-brand or mixed-category equipment purchases, third-party equipment lenders and banks can structure master credit facilities. Some alternative lenders offer revolving equipment credit lines where approved businesses can draw financing for new purchases without reapplying each time. Bundling equipment purchases can simplify the application process and sometimes improve terms compared to multiple separate applications. Consult your lender about blanket financing options when purchasing two or more pieces of equipment simultaneously.
How long does it take to get equipment financing approved?
Equipment financing approval timelines vary significantly by lender type. Alternative online lenders (Balboa Capital, TimePayment, Currency Capital) often provide same-day or next-day decisions for amounts up to $250,000 with minimal documentation — sometimes as little as a one-page application and 3 months of bank statements. OEM captive finance programs (equipment lenders, equipment lenders) typically approve within 1-3 business days for standard applications through the dealer. Traditional bank equipment finance divisions require 1-3 weeks for standard applications and up to 30-45 days for large or complex transactions. SBA 7(a) loans typically take 30-60 days, and SBA 504 loans take 45-90 days due to the additional CDC (Certified Development Company) processing step. For time-sensitive purchases, starting with OEM or alternative lenders provides the fastest path to equipment.
Do I need a business plan to get equipment financing?
A formal business plan is generally not required for equipment financing unless you are applying for an SBA loan or seeking bank financing as a startup with minimal operating history. Most established businesses (2+ years, consistent revenue) only need to provide tax returns, bank statements, and a personal financial statement. Startups and very new businesses benefit significantly from including a concise business plan — particularly one that addresses how the equipment will generate revenue, who the customers are, expected pricing, and how equipment payments fit into projected cash flow. Even a 2-3 page executive summary can meaningfully improve a startup lender's confidence. For SBA loans, a more comprehensive business plan with financial projections spanning 2-3 years is typically required as part of the formal application package.
What is the difference between a captive OEM lender and a third-party lender?
A captive OEM lender is the financing arm of an equipment manufacturer — examples include equipment lenders (Caterpillar), equipment lenders, equipment lenders, equipment lenders, and equipment lenders. These lenders exclusively finance their own brand's equipment, but often offer competitive promotional rates (sometimes 0% APR), deferred payment programs, and streamlined approvals through the dealership network. They are incentivized to finance their equipment to support dealer sales volume. Third-party lenders — banks, alternative lenders, equipment leasing companies — are not affiliated with any specific manufacturer and can finance any brand of equipment. They offer more flexibility for multi-brand purchases, used equipment purchases from private parties, and scenarios where OEM programs are unavailable or uncompetitive. Most equipment buyers should obtain quotes from both the OEM program and third-party lenders before committing.
Can foreign-made equipment (Chinese, European) be financed the same way as US-made equipment?
European-made equipment (Liebherr, Volvo CE, Wirtgen, DMG Mori, Trumpf) generally finances comparably to American-made equipment. Major lenders are comfortable with European brands because they have established US dealer networks, predictable resale values, and strong parts and service availability. Chinese-made equipment (XCMG, SANY, Lonking, LGMG) faces more financing restrictions: most major banks decline to finance Chinese construction equipment, specialty lenders are required, down payments are typically 15-25% (vs. 10-15% for Cat/Komatsu), interest rates run 1-3% higher, and maximum terms are sometimes shorter. The primary reason is lower and less predictable US resale values and thinner parts networks. XCMG and SANY have been actively building US dealer networks and OEM financing programs, which is gradually improving their financing accessibility for qualified buyers.
