Attachment Ecosystems

Bobcat Bob-Tach vs Cat Work Tool Carrier: What Contractors Need to Know

The attachment ecosystem is one of the most important practical considerations in the Bobcat vs Cat decision, because switching brands mid-fleet means either selling your attachment library or purchasing adapter plates for every piece.

Bobcat Bob-Tach is the most widely-used skid steer coupler standard in North America. The Bob-Tach system's dominance — Bobcat holds approximately 40% of the US skid steer market — has made it a de facto standard that hundreds of third-party attachment manufacturers design to first. Whether you need an auger, brush cutter, cold planer, grapple bucket, hydraulic breaker, pallet fork, planer, trencher, or sweeper, you will find more Bob-Tach-compatible options with better pricing competition than any other coupler system. Power Bob-Tach (hydraulic quick-attach) allows single-person attachment changes in under 30 seconds.

Cat Work Tool Carrier (CWT-S) is Cat's proprietary system, engineered specifically for Cat machines. Cat's attachment lineup is particularly strong for heavy earthwork applications — rock buckets, cold planers, high-reach demolition configurations, and specialty utility attachments that leverage Cat's hydraulic flow specifications. The CWT-S system is well-suited to contractors doing heavy-duty earthwork where Cat's attachment engineering matches the machine's capabilities. The trade-off is a smaller third-party attachment market compared to Bob-Tach, which means less price competition and fewer specialty option sources. Universal quick-attach (UQA) adapter plates can bridge coupler compatibility between brands, but must be sized to each attachment and may void warranty coverage in some cases. For contractors with an existing Bobcat attachment library, the adapter plate cost is a real but manageable consideration when evaluating a switch to Cat. Common Questions

Bobcat vs Cat Skid Steer Financing — FAQ

Is a Bobcat or Cat skid steer easier to finance for a startup contractor?
Both Bobcat and Caterpillar skid steers are among the most lender-friendly pieces of construction equipment available, due to their exceptional secondary market depth and brand recognition. For a startup contractor, the Bobcat S770 ($65,000–$85,000) is slightly easier to finance than the Cat 272D3 ($75,000–$95,000) because of its lower price point, approximately 40% US skid steer market share (which supports broad secondary demand), and equipment lenders's history of supporting new contractor purchases. The $10,000–$15,000 price advantage means a lower absolute loan amount, lower monthly payment, and less demanding down payment in dollar terms. equipment lenders is extremely well-funded with aggressive promotional offers, and established contractors with existing Cat dealer relationships may find Cat equally or more accessible. Either brand serves as excellent collateral for most equipment lenders.
What is the rated operating capacity difference between a Bobcat S770 and Cat 272D3?
The Bobcat S770 has a rated operating capacity (ROC) of 3,650 pounds, and the Cat 272D3 has a ROC of 3,600 pounds — a difference of just 50 pounds that is functionally insignificant in real-world operation. Both are classified as large-frame skid steers capable of demanding earthwork and heavy attachment tasks. ROC is defined at 50% of tipping load per SAE J818 standards, so both machines tip near 7,300 pounds. More meaningful operational differences lie in hydraulic flow (Cat 272D3 offers 35.4 gpm Hi-Flow vs. Bobcat S770's 32.4 gpm), engine power (Cat 3.8L 100hp vs. Bobcat 92hp Doosan), and attachment coupler compatibility. The Bobcat's slightly higher ROC rating is a minor advantage, while the Cat's higher hydraulic flow benefits high-demand attachments like cold planers and hydraulic breakers.
How do equipment lenders and equipment lenders compare for skid steer financing?
Both are captive OEM programs with competitive rates and frequent promotional offers. equipment lenders is backed by Caterpillar's larger balance sheet and runs 0% for 36 months on new Cat skid steers for qualified buyers — one of the most attractive promotional offers in the construction equipment industry. equipment lenders offers competitive 0% for 12–24 month periods integrated with the 900+ US Bobcat dealer network, providing convenient on-site approvals across more locations than Cat's approximately 400 US construction equipment locations. equipment lenders's dealer network is smaller for skid steers specifically but compensates with deeper promotional rate flexibility. Both programs are recognized by all major lenders for refinancing, and both provide fast approvals for well-qualified buyers. The practical difference for most contractors is access: Bobcat dealers are in more markets, but Cat's promotional rates are frequently more aggressive for qualified buyers.
What attachments work with Bobcat vs Cat skid steers?
Bobcat uses the Bob-Tach coupler system, and Cat uses the Cat Work Tool Carrier (CWC-S) system — these are proprietary and not universally interchangeable without adapter plates. Bobcat's Bob-Tach is the most widely-copied coupler design in North America, meaning hundreds of third-party manufacturers produce Bob-Tach-compatible attachments across augers, brush cutters, grapples, hydraulic breakers, planers, trenchers, sweepers, and more. The sheer volume of compatible options drives price competition and specialty availability. Cat's CWC-S attachment lineup is particularly strong for heavy earthwork — rock buckets, cold planers, and high-demand attachments matched to Cat's hydraulic flow specifications. If you already own a large Bob-Tach attachment inventory, switching to Cat means either selling the inventory or purchasing universal adapter plates for each attachment. That sunk cost should factor into your financing decision.
Which skid steer holds its value better — Bobcat or Caterpillar?
Cat skid steers hold their value slightly better than Bobcat machines at the 5-year mark, typically by 3–6 percentage points. Cat 272D3 machines at 5 years and 2,000–3,000 hours retain approximately 60–68% of purchase price at retail. Bobcat S770 machines at comparable age and hours retain 55–65%. The Cat premium reflects the brand's premium positioning and the preference of established contractors who view Cat as a quality indicator. Bobcat still posts excellent secondary market performance driven by its dominant 40% US market share and broad demand across residential, commercial, landscaping, and utility segments. Both brands dramatically outperform Case, John Deere, and secondary brands on resale consistency. The Cat resale premium partially offsets its higher purchase price over a 5-year ownership cycle, making the actual total cost of ownership comparison closer than the sticker price difference suggests.
Can I finance a used skid steer with 3,000+ hours?
Yes. Used Bobcat and Cat skid steers with 3,000+ hours can be financed, but with more conservative terms than new or low-hour machines. Lenders assess used skid steers on brand, condition, service history, and current market value rather than hours alone. A well-maintained Bobcat S650 or Cat 262D3 at 3,500 hours with documented service history and recently replaced tires or tracks is far more lendable than a neglected machine at 1,500 hours. Most lenders will finance well-maintained Bobcat and Cat skid steers up to 10,000–12,000 hours, though maximum loan terms shorten to 36–48 months for high-hour machines. Interest rates are typically 1–2 percentage points higher for high-hour machines. An independent equipment inspection report attached to the financing application significantly strengthens high-hour equipment applications by documenting the machine's actual condition to the lender.
Is a compact track loader (CTL) better than a skid steer for most contractors?
For most general contractors, a compact track loader (CTL) offers better all-around performance than a skid steer, though at a 15–25% higher purchase price. CTLs distribute machine weight across rubber tracks rather than point-loaded tires, dramatically reducing ground disturbance — critical for residential landscaping, turf restoration, and wet job sites where tire marks create remediation costs. CTLs provide better traction in mud, sand, and loose material, improving productivity in challenging conditions. The trade-off is that rubber tracks wear out ($4,000–$8,000 to replace) and CTLs cost more to purchase and maintain. Skid steers remain better for applications requiring frequent travel on paved surfaces or concrete where tracks would cause damage or wear rapidly. For contractors primarily working residential sites with nearby finished landscaping, the CTL's ground-protection advantage typically more than covers its price premium over a 5-year ownership cycle. The financing process for CTLs is essentially identical to skid steers — same lenders, same programs, same approval criteria.
What down payment do I need to finance a $75,000 skid steer?
For a new $75,000 Cat 272D3 or $65,000–$85,000 Bobcat S770, established contractors with 2+ years in business and 650+ credit can typically finance with 10–15% down ($7,500–$11,250 on a $75,000 machine) through equipment lenders, equipment lenders, or third-party lenders. equipment lenders frequently offers 0% for 36 months with no required down payment for well-qualified buyers — one of the most attractive financing structures available in construction equipment. For startup contractors under 24 months in business, 15–20% down is more realistic. Bobcat and Caterpillar skid steers are among the most lender-friendly equipment categories because of their strong secondary market — lenders know they can recover and resell the machine if needed. That collateral confidence supports more flexible approval standards than specialty or obscure equipment.
