OEM Financing Programs

John Deere Financial vs CNH Capital — Program Comparison

Both OEM programs are subsidiaries of major financial institutions and offer competitive rates, but they differ in program structure, promotional frequency, and ancillary products available to farm operators.

John Deere Financial is one of the largest agricultural finance companies in North America, with over $40 billion in managed receivables. Their FarmPlan revolving credit product is unique in the OEM space — it functions like a farm operating line that covers both equipment and input purchases. For large row crop tractors, John Deere Financial frequently offers 0% APR for 12–24 months at major agricultural trade shows and farm days. Their integration with the John Deere Operations Center telematics system allows lenders to access machine utilization data, which can support refinancing discussions.

CNH Capital (which finances both Case IH and New Holland equipment) is backed by the CNH Industrial financial services division and provides competitive term financing for Case IH equipment. CNH Capital runs regular 0% for 12–18 month promotional periods, particularly on model-year closeout equipment. For farmers purchasing multiple pieces of CNH equipment (tractor plus planter, tractor plus grain cart), CNH Capital can structure bundled financing that simplifies payments. Resale Value Analysis

Tractor Residual Values: Why They Matter for Financing

Residual value — the percentage of original purchase price a machine retains after a defined period — is one of the most important factors in equipment financing. Lenders use residual value estimates to set loan-to-value ratios, determine maximum loan amounts, and price interest rates. Equipment with strong residual values represents lower risk for lenders because the collateral retains its worth even if a loan goes delinquent.

John Deere 8R series tractors have among the highest residual values in the large row crop segment, driven by JD's dominant market share (approximately 35–40% of US tractor sales), deep secondary market, and extensive dealer network capable of retailing used machines. At five years and 2,500–3,500 hours, an 8R 280 typically brings 55–65% of its original purchase price at auction. On the dealer retail market, the same machine may bring 60–70%.

Case IH Magnum tractors are also strong performers on residual value, retaining 50–60% at five years under similar conditions. The gap versus John Deere narrows in strong Case IH regions (western Iowa, the Dakotas, parts of Kansas) where dealer density is comparable and local demand supports prices. Common Questions

John Deere vs Case IH Tractor Financing — FAQ

Is John Deere or Case IH easier to finance?
John Deere is generally easier to finance in most US markets due to the sheer size of its dealer network — over 4,200 US locations versus approximately 2,800 for Case IH. More dealers means more local competition for your business, easier access to OEM promotional financing, and faster service turnaround that lenders view favorably when assessing collateral risk. John Deere Financial offers a broader range of tailored ag financing products including operating lines tied to crop revenue. That said, CNH Capital financing for Case IH is widely accepted and competitive; in strong Case IH dealer regions, financing access is largely comparable.
What is the resale value difference between JD and Case IH?
John Deere row crop tractors consistently command a 5–10% premium on the used market compared to equivalent Case IH models. A John Deere 8R 280 typically retains 68–75% of its purchase price at 3 years and 55–65% at 5 years. A Case IH Magnum 280 of the same age retains approximately 63–70% at 3 years and 50–60% at 5 years. This gap is well documented in Ritchie Bros. and Purple Wave auction data. For lenders, higher residual values mean lower loan-to-value risk, which can translate into better financing rates for JD borrowers. Case IH machines are excellent — the resale gap reflects market share, not quality differences.
Can a new farm operation finance a large row crop tractor?
Yes, but startup farm financing for a $285,000–$320,000 row crop tractor requires strong preparation. Lenders want to see at least 20–30% down payment, a personal credit score of 680+, documentation of land ownership or long-term lease, a detailed business plan with projected cash flow, and prior farming experience. The USDA Farm Service Agency Beginning Farmer loan program provides a pathway for new operators who lack the balance sheet that commercial lenders require. John Deere Financial's FarmPlan product also includes programs for beginning farmers that may offer more flexible collateral requirements than conventional lenders. Starting with a used tractor at a lower price point is a common strategy for reducing the initial financing hurdle.
What does John Deere Financial offer that CNH Capital doesn't?
John Deere Financial's FarmPlan revolving credit account is a meaningful differentiator — it functions as an integrated operating line covering both equipment and input purchases (seed, chemicals, fuel) on a single account structure. CNH Capital does not offer an equivalent integrated farm operating credit product. John Deere Financial also integrates with the John Deere Operations Center telematics platform, allowing machine utilization data to inform lending decisions. JD Financial tends to run 0% APR promotions for 12–24 months more frequently than CNH Capital's typical 12–18 month window. Both are strong, well-funded programs; the differences are meaningful for large operations that also need working capital alongside equipment financing.
Which tractor holds value better — John Deere 8R or Case IH Magnum?
The John Deere 8R consistently outperforms the Case IH Magnum on resale value, typically by a 5–8 percentage point margin at the 5-year mark. JD 8R series machines in similar hours and condition sell for $15,000–$30,000 more than comparable Case IH Magnums at major farm equipment auctions. The reasons are structural — JD's dominant market share, 4,200+ dealers who can retail used machines, and deeper auction liquidity. From a financing standpoint, lenders assign lower collateral risk to JD equipment, which can help borrowers secure better terms. The Case IH Magnum is an excellent machine; the resale gap reflects market dynamics rather than any deficiency in quality or reliability.
What credit score do I need to finance a $300,000 tractor?
John Deere Financial and CNH Capital generally require a minimum 640–660 personal credit score for competitive rate programs on large row crop tractors. For the best promotional rate offers (0% for 12–24 months), you will typically need 700+. Farm credit associations often work with scores in the 600–640 range for borrowers with strong farm income and land assets. Alternative equipment lenders specializing in ag can sometimes approve scores as low as 580 for established operations with verifiable revenue. A 20% down payment on a $300,000 tractor ($60,000 down) significantly strengthens any application regardless of credit tier. Is the Case IH Magnum worth considering over the John Deere 8R?
Absolutely. The Case IH Magnum 280 is a purpose-built, highly capable large row crop tractor that typically costs $20,000–$30,000 less than a comparably-equipped John Deere 8R 280. The FPT Cursor 9 engine is proven and reliable, and the AFS Connect precision agriculture platform is genuinely competitive with JD's Operations Center for guidance, planting, and yield mapping. Farmers in regions with strong Case IH dealer density — particularly the Midwest and Great Plains — often report comparable service access and parts availability to their JD neighbors. The lower purchase price means meaningfully lower monthly payments, which matters for cash-flow-sensitive operations. For value-focused buyers, the Magnum is a compelling choice that should always be quoted alongside the JD 8R.
How does tractor financing differ from construction equipment financing?
Agricultural tractor financing has several unique characteristics. Farm credit institutions — Farm Credit Services, AgFirst, CoBank — are cooperative lenders existing specifically to serve agricultural borrowers, often at rates below conventional commercial lenders. These institutions understand seasonal income patterns and can structure loan payments around harvest and planting cash flow rather than requiring equal monthly installments, which is uncommon in construction equipment lending. Equipment residual values in agriculture are also tied to commodity prices in ways that construction equipment is not — a major commodity price downturn can affect both farm income and used tractor values simultaneously. USDA FSA loan guarantee programs also provide a backstop for qualified farm borrowers with no direct equivalent in the construction sector.
