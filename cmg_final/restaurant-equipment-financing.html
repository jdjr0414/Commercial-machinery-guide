Requirements for Restaurant Equipment Financing

NSF Certification

All commercial food contact equipment must carry NSF International (NSF/ANSI 2, 4, 7) certification. Health departments in every state require NSF certification for equipment approval. Non-certified equipment may be rejected at inspection, jeopardizing restaurant opening. Lenders increasingly require NSF documentation, especially for startup restaurant financing where equipment failure could force closure.

Health Department Permits

Restaurants must hold a valid food service establishment permit from the local health department. New equipment installations typically require a health department inspection. Walk-in cooler and exhaust hood installations often require building permits in addition to health department approval. Pre-opening inspections by local health authorities are a condition of restaurant opening and indirectly affect lender confidence in the restaurant's ability to operate.

Credit Requirements

Established restaurants (2+ years) with 680+ credit qualify for standard rates (5.9%–9%). New restaurants need 700+ personal credit, a solid business plan, signed lease agreement, and typically 20–30% down. Restaurant-specific lenders (Marlin Business Services, Ascentium Capital, Balboa Capital) are more comfortable with foodservice industry risk profiles than general commercial lenders.

Fire Code Compliance

Commercial cooking equipment requiring grease exhaust must have a UL 300-listed fire suppression system in the exhaust hood. Local fire marshals inspect commercial kitchens for code compliance. Ansul (Tyco Fire Products) and Kidde are the leading fire suppression system manufacturers. Suppression system installation ($3,000–$15,000) can often be bundled into restaurant equipment financing.

Business Structure

Restaurant equipment lenders prefer borrowers operating as LLCs or corporations with an EIN and dedicated business bank account. Sole proprietors may face higher rates and stricter requirements. Some lenders require personal guarantees from all owners with 20%+ equity stake in the restaurant entity. Restaurant groups with multiple locations often qualify for better rates than single-unit operators.

Insurance Requirements

Commercial property insurance covering all financed equipment at replacement value with lender as loss payee is required. Restaurant businesses also need commercial general liability ($1M minimum), liquor liability if serving alcohol, workers' compensation for employees, and business interruption insurance. Lenders may require proof of all applicable coverages, particularly for large kitchen buildout loans over $100,000.

Restaurant Income Potential

Full-Service Restaurant

$80,000–$500,000/year net

Full-service restaurants gross $500,000–$3M/year in a typical unit depending on size and concept. Food cost runs 28–35%, labor 30–35%, and occupancy 8–12%, leaving EBITDA margins of 10–20% for successful operations. A $1M/year restaurant netting 15% generates $150,000 in owner income — improving to $200,000–$400,000+ as the restaurant reaches 2–3x revenue through second locations or catering programs.

Quick Service Restaurant (QSR)

$60,000–$300,000/year net

Fast casual and QSR restaurants achieve higher throughput per square foot with lower labor costs than full-service concepts. Unit-level EBITDA for successful QSR locations runs 15–25% of gross revenues. A $800,000/year fast casual unit with 20% margins generates $160,000 in unit-level profit. Multi-unit operators scaling to 3–5 locations can achieve total owner income of $300,000–$700,000/year from the portfolio.

Catering & Ghost Kitchen

$100,000–$400,000/year net

Catering businesses using commercial kitchen equipment generate $40–$120/person for event catering. A caterer doing 150 events/year averaging 100 guests at $60/person generates $900,000/year in gross revenue. Ghost kitchens (delivery-only) operating from shared or owned commercial kitchen space achieve lower overhead than traditional restaurants, with 25–40% EBITDA margins for well-run operations targeting high-demand delivery markets.

Restaurant Equipment Financing FAQ

How much does restaurant equipment financing cost per month?
Monthly restaurant equipment payments range from approximately $100 to $10,000+ depending on the equipment package and loan term. A $25,000 basic startup kitchen package at 7% over 60 months costs about $495/month. A $100,000 full commercial kitchen buildout at 7% over 60 months runs about $1,980/month. A $250,000 complete high-volume restaurant kitchen at 7% over 72 months costs approximately $4,275/month. Equipment-only financing (excluding furniture and leasehold improvements) typically runs $500–$3,000/month for a standard restaurant.
What restaurant equipment can be financed?
Virtually all commercial kitchen equipment can be financed including commercial ovens and ranges ($3,000–$25,000), commercial refrigerators and walk-in coolers ($2,000–$40,000), commercial dishwashers ($3,000–$25,000), deep fryers ($2,000–$15,000), commercial mixers ($2,000–$15,000), espresso machines and coffee systems ($3,000–$25,000), POS systems ($2,000–$15,000), ventilation hoods ($3,000–$25,000), and ice machines ($2,000–$12,000). Soft costs like installation and delivery can sometimes be bundled into the loan amount.
Can a startup restaurant get equipment financing?
Yes, though startup restaurant financing is more challenging than established business financing. Lenders specializing in restaurant equipment understand the industry's high failure rate and typically require 700+ personal credit, a detailed business plan, signed lease agreement for the restaurant space, and 20–30% down payment. Some SBA 7(a) loans can fund restaurant startups with lower down requirements. Equipment leasing programs are often more accessible for restaurant startups, as operating leases focus more on equipment value than borrower history.
Is it better to lease or finance restaurant equipment?
Leasing is popular for restaurant equipment because it preserves working capital (lower monthly payments), allows upgrades as technology improves, and provides tax advantages (full lease payment deductible). Financing (purchasing) is better when you want to own the equipment, take Section 179 deductions, or plan to use the equipment for 10+ years. For a new restaurant, leasing commercial refrigeration and cooking equipment while financing fixed infrastructure (walk-ins, exhaust hoods) is a common hybrid approach that balances cash flow with long-term asset building.
What credit score is needed for restaurant equipment financing?
Established restaurants (2+ years, profitable) with 680+ credit typically qualify for standard equipment financing with competitive rates. New restaurants need 700+ personal credit given the industry's risk profile. Restaurant-specific lenders (Marlin Business Services, Balboa Capital, Crest Capital) are more familiar with restaurant industry cash flows and may approve borrowers that general equipment lenders decline. SBA 7(a) loans backed by the Small Business Administration have more flexible credit criteria for restaurant financing.
What are the top commercial kitchen equipment brands?
Top commercial kitchen equipment brands include Hobart Corporation (Troy, Ohio) for mixers, slicers, and dishwashers; Vulcan Equipment (Louisville, Kentucky) for ranges and ovens; Manitowoc Ice (Manitowoc, Wisconsin) for ice machines; True Manufacturing (O'Fallon, Missouri) for refrigeration; Middleby Corporation (Elgin, Illinois) brand portfolio including Blodgett, Pitco, and others; Welbilt Inc. (New Port Richey, Florida) for Convotherm, Manitowoc, and Merco brands; and Hatco Corporation (Milwaukee, Wisconsin) for food warming and holding equipment.
How long can I finance restaurant equipment?
Restaurant equipment loan terms range from 24 to 84 months. Individual pieces of equipment ($3,000–$15,000) typically finance over 24–36 months. Complete kitchen packages ($30,000–$150,000) commonly finance over 48–60 months. Large buildouts over $200,000 can qualify for 60–84 month terms through SBA loans or specialty restaurant equipment lenders. Refrigeration equipment, which has a useful life of 10–15 years, supports longer financing terms than cooking equipment with more variable maintenance histories.
What health and safety requirements apply to restaurant equipment financing?
Restaurant equipment must meet NSF International (National Sanitation Foundation) certification standards for food equipment safety. NSF certification is required by virtually all health departments for commercial food service equipment. Equipment must also comply with UL (Underwriters Laboratories) electrical safety standards and local fire codes for commercial cooking equipment (grease hood exhaust, suppression systems). Lenders financing restaurant equipment expect NSF-certified equipment, as non-certified equipment may face health department rejection that endangers the restaurant's ability to operate.
