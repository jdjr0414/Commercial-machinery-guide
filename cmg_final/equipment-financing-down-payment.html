Common Questions

Equipment Financing Down Payment — FAQ

Can I use an equipment trade-in as a down payment?
Yes. Trading in existing equipment is one of the most effective ways to reduce cash required at closing. The dealer applies the trade-in value directly to the purchase price, reducing the amount financed. For example, a $25,000 trade-in toward a $150,000 machine effectively covers a 16.7% down payment with no cash out of pocket. Always get a pre-trade appraisal from multiple dealers to ensure you receive fair market value before agreeing to terms.
What are the requirements for 0% down equipment financing?
Zero-down equipment financing through OEM programs (equipment lenders, equipment lenders, Haas Financial) typically requires: 680–720+ personal credit score, 2+ years in business with documented revenue, strong bank statement history, new equipment from the current model year, and purchasing from an authorized dealer. These promotions are periodic — typically at trade shows or year-end dealer pushes. Non-OEM lenders occasionally offer 0% down to exceptionally qualified buyers for equipment with strong collateral values.
How does down payment size affect my interest rate?
A larger down payment generally results in a lower interest rate because it reduces the lender's loan-to-value exposure. Going from 10% to 20% down may reduce APR by 0.25–0.75% depending on lender. More meaningfully, the lower principal from a larger down payment dramatically reduces total interest paid. On a $300,000 machine at 7% over 60 months, the difference between 10% and 20% down is approximately $594/month and over $35,000 total in financing costs saved. See our how equipment financing works guide for full rate analysis.
Can I use a credit card for the down payment on equipment financing?
Most equipment lenders prohibit using a credit card for the required down payment because it creates additional debt simultaneously with the equipment loan. Lenders often verify the source of down payment funds through bank statements. For smaller equipment purchases under $25,000, some business owners use a business credit card to purchase outright, intending to pay it off quickly — but for larger financed equipment, down payment must typically come from savings, trade-in equity, or a HELOC.
Can I use a HELOC for an equipment down payment?
Yes, a HELOC is a valid down payment source and most equipment lenders accept it. The HELOC funds appear as cash in your bank account. The personal risk is real — you are pledging home equity to fund business equipment. If the business struggles, the HELOC payment still comes due. This approach works best when you have strong confidence in the equipment's revenue-generating ability. Always confirm with your equipment lender that borrowed down payment funds are acceptable before proceeding.
What is the down payment for medical equipment financing?
Established medical practices (2+ years, 660+ credit) frequently qualify for 0–10% down on diagnostic equipment through specialty medical lenders or OEM programs (GE Healthcare Capital, Philips Capital). Startup medical practices typically need 20–30% down. Dental equipment through specialized lenders (Patterson Dental Financial, Benco) often offers 0% down for new practices due to the profession's predictable income. Medical equipment financing is generally more favorable than most industrial equipment categories.
What is the minimum down payment for crane financing?
Crane financing typically requires 0–20% down depending on credit (0% available for qualified borrowers) for established contractors and 25–35% for newer businesses. Cranes are specialized, high-value equipment with a narrower secondary market than general construction equipment, which is why lenders require more down. Mobile cranes ($500,000–$2,000,000+) from Liebherr, Manitowoc, Grove, or Tadano require strong financials and usually 20%+ down. Smaller pick-and-carry cranes ($80,000–$250,000) are more accessible for qualified borrowers.
Does a larger down payment always reduce the interest rate?
Not always to a significant degree, but a larger down payment reliably reduces total interest paid because the loan principal is lower. Some lenders use risk-tiered pricing where 20%+ down triggers a lower rate tier. Others set rates based purely on credit score regardless of down payment. The guaranteed benefit of a larger down payment is reduced monthly payments and total cost of financing, even if the rate remains unchanged. Always compare total cost of financing — not just the rate — when evaluating down payment strategy.
