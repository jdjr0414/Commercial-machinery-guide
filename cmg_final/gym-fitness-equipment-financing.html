Licensing & Regulatory Requirements

Gym & Fitness Center Business Licensing

Commercial gyms and fitness centers face several licensing and regulatory requirements before opening. Understanding these upfront prevents costly delays.

  • Business License: Required in all states and most municipalities. General business operating license from city/county clerk.
  • Certificate of Occupancy: Required for commercial fitness space. Building must pass structural, electrical, and fire code inspections.
  • ADA Compliance (Title III): Gyms are public accommodations under ADA. Requires accessible entrances, parking, restrooms, equipment areas, and signage. Retrofitting non-compliant spaces can add $10,000–$100,000 to buildout.
  • State Fitness Facility Registration / Bonding: Many states (California, Florida, New York, Illinois, others) require fitness facilities selling prepaid memberships to register with the state AG and post a surety bond ($25,000–$100,000). Verify your state's Prepaid Entertainment Contract Act or Health Club Act requirements.
  • CPR/AED Requirements: Most states require at least one staff member CPR/AED certified on every shift. Some states (California, New York) require AED units on-site by law.
  • Health Department Permits: Required if operating pools, hot tubs, steam rooms, or food/beverage service. Pool inspections are routine annual events.
  • Zoning Approval: High-traffic commercial gym use must be permitted in your zoning district. Confirm parking ratios (often 1 space per 200 sq ft for gyms) and loading requirements.
  • Music Licensing: Playing music in a gym requires ASCAP, BMI, and SESAC licenses. Annual cost: $400–$2,000 depending on facility size.

Common Questions

Commercial Gym Equipment Financing — FAQ

What is the monthly payment for a full gym buildout?
A standard gym buildout at $200,000–$350,000 financed over 60 months at 7% APR runs approximately $3,960–$6,930/month. A boutique studio buildout at $50,000–$150,000 over 60 months runs $990–$2,970/month. A full-service health club at $400,000–$800,000 over 60 months at 7% runs $7,921–$15,841/month. Down payments of 10–15% reduce the financed amount. Many lenders offer equipment-only financing that excludes leasehold improvements, so the equipment portion alone may be $30,000–$200,000 depending on buildout scope.
Should I lease or finance commercial gym equipment?
For established gyms planning to hold equipment 5+ years, financing (ownership) is almost always better — you build equity, take full Section 179 deduction, and avoid equipment return clauses. Operating leases make more sense for boutique studios wanting to refresh equipment every 3–4 years without residual risk, or for gyms in startup mode where preserving cash flow is critical. Life Fitness and Precor both offer lease programs through their dealer networks. See our full analysis at equipment financing vs. lease guide.
What brands are most recognized by gym equipment lenders?
Life Fitness, Precor, Technogym, and Matrix (Johnson Health Tech) are the most recognized brands by commercial equipment lenders. They have established secondary markets, strong dealer networks, and documented resale values. Hammer Strength and Cybex are also well-recognized. Niche brands like Keiser, Concept2, and Rogue Fitness are known to specialty lenders. Unknown or imported fitness brands may require larger down payments (20–30%) and independent appraisal.
How does gym equipment depreciate?
Commercial gym equipment typically depreciates 15–25% in the first year and 10–15% annually thereafter. Cardio equipment depreciates faster due to wear and electronics — a $10,000 commercial treadmill is worth $3,000–$5,000 after 5 years. Strength equipment (racks, plates, dumbbells) holds value much better — often 60–70% after 5 years. For tax purposes, gym equipment falls under 5-year MACRS depreciation, or you can take full Section 179 expensing in year one.
Can I finance used gym equipment?
Yes. Used commercial gym equipment from recognized brands (Life Fitness, Precor, Technogym, Matrix) can be financed with most specialty equipment lenders. Typical requirements: equipment under 10–12 years old, documented appraisal or purchase invoice, equipment in working condition. Rates on used equipment run 1–3% higher than new. Down payments are typically 15–20% for used vs. 10–15% for new. See our new vs. used equipment financing guide for full details.
What credit score do I need for gym equipment financing?
Most commercial lenders require 640–680+ personal credit for gym equipment financing. OEM programs through Life Fitness Financial or Precor dealer financing may require 660+. Alternative lenders work with 550–620+ for established gyms with 2+ years of revenue. Startup gym financing is the most challenging — lenders want to see 700+ credit, 0–20% down (0% available for qualified borrowers), and a signed lease. See our equipment financing credit requirements guide.
Can I use an SBA loan to finance a gym startup?
Yes. SBA 7(a) loans and SBA 504 loans both work for gym startups. SBA 7(a) loans (up to $5M) can cover equipment, leasehold improvements, and working capital. For a $300,000 gym buildout, an SBA 7(a) loan with 10% down requires approximately $30,000 equity injection. Processing takes 60–90 days. See our startup equipment financing guide for more detail.
How long does commercial gym equipment last?
Commercial-grade treadmills from Life Fitness or Precor typically last 10–15 years with proper maintenance (belt replacements every 3–5 years). Ellipticals and bikes last 10–15 years. Strength equipment (plates, barbells, dumbbells, racks) can last 20–30+ years. Selectorized strength machines last 10–20 years. Cable machines require bearing and cable replacements every 5–8 years. Proper preventive maintenance programs are essential — most OEMs offer service contracts at $50–$150/unit/year for commercial clients.
