RADIOFREQUENCY (RF) SHIELDING — THE CAGE:

An MRI machine generates extremely powerful radiofrequency (RF) signals. Without RF shielding, the MRI interferes with every electronic device within hundreds of feet — cell phones, computers, pacemakers, other medical equipment. Federal law and physics require that MRI machines be installed in a shielded room called a Faraday cage.

RF shielding basics:
- A Faraday cage is a room with continuous copper or aluminum mesh embedded in the walls, floor, and ceiling
- Every penetration (HVAC ducts, electrical conduits, plumbing, doors, windows) must be shielded with RF filters or waveguides
- The MRI manufacturer specifies the required attenuation level (typically 90-100 dB)
- Failure to properly shield results in image degradation and regulatory non-compliance

RF shielding cost:
- Small room (1.5T MRI, 600-800 sq ft): $80,000-$150,000 for shielding alone
- Medium room (3T MRI, 800-1,200 sq ft): $120,000-$220,000
- Large room with bore room + control room + equipment room: $200,000-$400,000
- RF shielding contractors: ETS-Lindgren (Cedar Park TX), Amuneal Manufacturing (Philadelphia PA), Magnetic Resonance Safety Services (MRSS)
- Installation timeline: 4-8 weeks after site preparation

MAGNETIC FIELD CONSIDERATIONS — THE FRINGE FIELD:

MRI magnets have a powerful magnetic field that extends beyond the magnet itself. This fringe field creates a safety exclusion zone where ferromagnetic objects (tools, wheelchairs, oxygen tanks) can become dangerous projectiles.

Fringe field planning:
- 5 Gauss line: the boundary where ferromagnetic objects become dangerous. Must be contained within the controlled access zone.
- 0.5 mT line (5 Gauss): must not extend into areas where people with pacemakers might be
- For a 1.5T MRI, the 5 Gauss line extends approximately 15-20 feet from the bore center without shielding
- Active shielding (built into modern magnets) and passive shielding (steel plates in walls) reduce the fringe field
- Magnetic shielding (NOT the same as RF shielding): heavy steel plate in walls — cost $50,000-$250,000 depending on situation
- Site planning requires a Gauss map from the MRI manufacturer

STRUCTURAL REQUIREMENTS:

MRI machines are extremely heavy. A 1.5T MRI weighs 4,000-10,000 lbs. A 3T can weigh 8,000-15,000 lbs. The floor must support this.

Structural requirements:
- Floor loading: typically 300-500 lbs/sq ft required, often requiring structural engineering review
- Floor reinforcement: $20,000-$100,000 depending on existing structure
- New concrete slab may be required: $30,000-$80,000
- Vibration isolation: MRI is sensitive to vibration (road traffic, HVAC equipment, construction). Vibration mounts or floating floor: $15,000-$50,000
- Ceiling height: minimum 10 ft, often 12-14 ft required for installation crane access
- Delivery access: MRI magnets require a large access corridor (typically 5 ft wide, no turns less than 90 degrees, adequate floor loading throughout path)

QUENCH PIPE:

A superconducting MRI magnet contains 1,500-2,000 liters of liquid helium at -452°F. In an emergency (quench), this helium vaporizes rapidly, expanding 760x in volume. A quench pipe vents this gas safely outside the building.

Quench pipe requirements:
- Required by MRI manufacturers and building codes
- Typically 8-10 inch diameter pipe routed from the magnet room to outside the building
- Path must be as short and straight as possible
- Penetrations through walls must be sealed and fire-rated
- Cost: $10,000-$40,000 depending on routing complexity

HVAC REQUIREMENTS:

MRI machines generate significant heat and require precise temperature control:
- Room temperature: 65-75°F, ±2°F
- Humidity: 40-60% RH
- Dedicated HVAC system: $20,000-$80,000
- Must be isolated from vibration
- All HVAC components within the 5 Gauss line must be non-ferromagnetic

ELECTRICAL REQUIREMENTS:

- Dedicated electrical service: typically 60-100 amp, 3-phase, 208V or 480V
- Clean power — UPS or power conditioner often required: $10,000-$50,000
- All electrical conduit within the shielded room must be non-ferromagnetic (aluminum or copper)
- Waveguide filters on all electrical penetrations
- Electrician must be certified/trained in MRI installation work
- Electrical work: $30,000-$80,000

CONTROL ROOM AND TECH ROOM:

The MRI is not just the magnet — it requires adjacent spaces:
- Magnet/bore room: where the patient and machine are
- Control room: where the technologist sits, with computer equipment, monitors, and viewing windows (special leaded glass that also provides RF shielding)
- Equipment room: houses the electronics, cooling equipment, and RF amplifiers
- Total space requirement: typically 800-1,500 sq ft for the full suite

Control room buildout: $40,000-$120,000

ONGOING COSTS:

After installation, an MRI has significant ongoing costs that affect the financing decision:
- Helium refill: modern magnets are "zero boil-off" and rarely need refill, but older magnets: $20,000-$40,000/refill
- Service contract: $150,000-$300,000/year for full-service coverage (parts and labor). Without a service contract, a single component failure can cost $50,000-$500,000
- Coil replacement: receiver coils wear out and cost $10,000-$80,000 each
- Software upgrades: $20,000-$100,000 per major upgrade
- These ongoing costs must be factored into the financing decision

ADD SECTION: Starting a Medical Imaging Business — The Full Picture

Write 700+ words on what it actually takes to start an MRI or imaging center.

BUSINESS MODEL OPTIONS:

1. Hospital-affiliated: Most radiologists and imaging centers work through hospital systems. Lower capital required, built-in patient referrals, but lower autonomy.

2. Independent imaging center: Stand-alone facility offering MRI, CT, X-ray, ultrasound. High capital requirement, full control over operations. This is the model this section focuses on.

3. Mobile MRI: MRI machine mounted in a trailer that travels to multiple sites. Truck-mounted MRI systems cost $1M-$3M (trailer + equipment). Eliminates the room construction cost but limits accessibility.

4. Radiologist-owned: Radiologists form a group, purchase equipment, and professional fees go directly to the practice.

CERTIFICATE OF NEED (CON) — THE BIGGEST OBSTACLE:

35 states have Certificate of Need laws that require government approval before installing certain medical equipment, including MRI machines. This is the most significant barrier to entry for independent imaging.

CON states for MRI (as of 2024): Alabama, Alaska, Arkansas, Connecticut, Delaware, DC, Florida, Georgia, Hawaii, Illinois, Indiana, Iowa, Kansas, Kentucky, Louisiana, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, Michigan, Minnesota, Mississippi, Missouri, Montana, Nebraska, Nevada, New Hampshire, New Jersey, New York, North Carolina, North Dakota, Ohio, Oklahoma, Oregon, Rhode Island, South Carolina, Tennessee, Virginia, Washington, West Virginia

States WITHOUT CON (easier entry): Arizona, California, Colorado, Idaho, New Mexico, North Dakota (limited), Pennsylvania, Texas, Utah, Wisconsin

CON process: Apply to state health department, demonstrate community need, public comment period, approval — typically 6-18 months and $10,000-$50,000 in legal/consulting fees, with no guarantee of approval.

LICENSING AND CERTIFICATION REQUIREMENTS:

- Radiation Safety Officer (RSO): Required by NRC regulations. Must supervise all radiation-producing equipment.
- Radiologic Technologist (RT(MR)): ARRT-certified MRI technologist required for each shift
- Radiologist physician: MD with radiology residency and fellowship; must read and interpret studies
- ACR Accreditation: American College of Radiology accreditation — required for Medicare reimbursement of MRI studies. Annual review process.
- State imaging facility license: required in most states; varies in requirements
- Medicare/Medicaid enrollment: required for insurance reimbursement; separate enrollment for facility and physician
- CLIA (Clinical Laboratory Improvement Amendments): if performing any lab-adjacent services
- Joint Commission accreditation: optional but preferred by referring physicians

STAFFING REQUIREMENTS (independent imaging center):
- Radiologist: $350,000-$550,000/year salary (or group ownership model)
- MRI Technologist: $70,000-$95,000/year (need minimum 1 per shift, 2 for safety)
- Front desk/scheduling: $35,000-$55,000/year
- Billing specialist: $45,000-$65,000/year (or outsource)
- Site/facility manager: $55,000-$85,000/year
- Total staffing (basic operation): $600,000-$900,000/year

REVENUE MODEL:

MRI reimbursement varies significantly by payer:
- Medicare: $300-$900 per MRI study depending on body part and magnet strength
- Commercial insurance: $400-$1,500 per study
- Self-pay: $400-$2,500 (full price)
- Average blended rate in most markets: $500-$800 per study

Volume needed to break even (rough model):
- Total annual cost (equipment financing + shielding + staffing + overhead): $1,200,000-$2,500,000
- At $600/study average: need 2,000-4,200 studies/year to break even
- At 10 studies/day: 2,500 studies/year = achievable
- Full utilization (18+ studies/day): $2.7M-$5.4M annual revenue potential

TIMELINE TO OPEN:

Month 1-3: Site selection, CON application (if required), architectural plans
Month 3-6: CON review, construction permits, structural/shielding engineering
Month 6-12: Construction, shielding installation, equipment order (lead time 6-12 months for new MRI)
Month 12-18: Equipment installation and commissioning (4-8 weeks)
Month 15-20: ACR accreditation, state licensing, Medicare enrollment
Month 18-24: Open for patient care

TOTAL STARTUP COST (independent 1.5T imaging center):
- Equipment (1.5T MRI + X-ray + ultrasound): $800,000-$2,000,000
- Room construction and shielding: $200,000-$500,000
- Buildout (waiting room, reception, restrooms): $100,000-$300,000
- IT systems (RIS, PACS, EMR, billing): $50,000-$150,000
- Initial working capital: $200,000-$400,000
- Licensing, accreditation, legal: $30,000-$80,000
- **Total: $1,380,000-$3,430,000**

FINANCING APPROACH:
- Equipment: OEM financing through Siemens Financial, GE Capital, Philips Healthcare Finance or independent specialty lender
- Buildout/shielding: SBA 504 loan (best option for fixed assets attached to real estate), commercial construction loan, or conventional commercial mortgage
- Working capital: SBA 7(a), business line of credit
- Many imaging centers use a combination of all three
