How to Start a CNC Machine Shop

Complete startup guide covering equipment costs, licensing, facility requirements, finding customers, and financing. Total investment: $150,000–$800,000 for a serious job shop.

Quick Answer: Starting a basic CNC job shop requires $150,000–$800,000 total investment covering 1–3 CNC machines, tooling, workholding, metrology equipment, and working capital. No specific machinist license is required in most states, but OSHA machine guarding compliance is mandatory. The most important factor is customer acquisition — most new shops succeed by working as subcontractors for established manufacturers before pursuing direct contracts.

Step 1: Define Your Niche

The most important decision before buying any equipment is choosing your market niche. Your customer target dictates every equipment decision — a shop making simple aluminum brackets needs a very different setup than a shop making titanium aerospace components.

Job Shop vs. Production Shop

A job shop makes low-to-medium volumes of many different part types for multiple customers. High mix, low volume. Equipment must be versatile. A production shop makes high volumes of a few part designs, often for a single customer or industry. Equipment can be dedicated and optimized. Most startups begin as job shops — it requires less customer concentration and lets you learn which work is most profitable.

Step 3: Facility Requirements

CNC machines are heavy industrial equipment with specific facility requirements. Signing a lease before verifying these requirements is one of the most common and costly startup mistakes.

  • Floor space: 1,000–3,000 sq ft per machine minimum, including material storage, work area, and office. A 2-machine shop needs 3,000–5,000 sq ft total.
  • Concrete floor: 6-inch reinforced slab minimum. A large VMC weighs 15,000–30,000 lbs — thin concrete or raised floors are not acceptable.
  • Power: 3-phase 208V or 480V required. Single-phase residential power will not run CNC machine tools. Verify before signing any lease.
  • Compressed air: Shop air at 90–120 PSI required for tool changers, chip conveyors, and blow-off guns.
  • Crane or forklift access: Essential for loading heavy fixtures and parts. A $15,000–$25,000 shop crane or forklift access solves this.
  • Lighting: Bright LED lighting throughout (3,000 lux minimum at work surfaces). Dark shops cause inspection errors.
  • Ventilation: Coolant mist extraction is an OSHA requirement. Budget $5,000–$15,000 for mist collectors — one per machine.
  • Loading dock or drive-in access: Critical for receiving raw material and shipping finished parts.

Step 4: Licensing and Compliance

Business License

State and local business license required in all jurisdictions. Also register for sales tax collection in your state. Cost: $50–$500.

OSHA Machine Guarding (29 CFR 1910.212)

Mandatory federal requirement. All rotating parts, cutting tools, and pinch points must be guarded. OSHA inspections can result in fines up to $15,625 per violation.

OSHA Lockout/Tagout (29 CFR 1910.147)

Mandatory procedure for controlling hazardous energy during maintenance. Requires written procedures, annual training, and equipment-specific LOTO devices.

EPA Coolant Disposal

Metalworking fluids are regulated waste. Cannot be dumped in drains. Requires licensed coolant recycler pickup. Most shops pay $500–$2,000/year for coolant disposal.

ISO 9001 Certification

Not legally required but expected by aerospace, medical, and automotive customers. Cost: $10,000–$25,000 for initial certification. Required within 1–2 years of targeting these markets.

AS9100D / ITAR

AS9100D required for aerospace contracts. ITAR registration ($2,500 fee) required for defense-related manufacturing. These typically aren't needed until year 2–3 of operation.

Step 5: Finding Your First Customers

Equipment is the easy part. Customers are the hard part. Most CNC machine shops that fail do so because of insufficient customer acquisition, not technical problems.

  • Subcontracting first: The fastest path to cash flow. Contact established machine shops in your area and offer overflow capacity. You handle the machining; they handle the customer relationship. Lower margins but guaranteed work from day one.
  • Thomasnet.com: The original industrial buyer directory. List your capabilities, materials, tolerances, and certifications. Thousands of OEM buyers search here daily.
  • Xometry and MFG.com: Online job shop marketplaces where OEMs post RFQs. Xometry operates its own network and can provide steady quote opportunities to new shops.
  • Local cold calling: Drive through your nearest industrial parks and introduce yourself to purchasing managers. In-person relationships build faster than online listing.
  • SAM.gov registration: Government contractors often need domestic machining subcontractors. Register your NAICS codes (332710 for machine shops) and pursue small business set-aside contracts.
  • Industry associations: The National Tooling and Machining Association (NTMA) has chapters in most regions and provides buyer connections and technical resources.

Step 6: Staffing

A solo operator can run 1–2 machines effectively. To grow beyond that, you need at least one additional machinist. The US machining workforce is aging — experienced CNC operators earn $22–$38/hour, and setup machinists command $35–$55/hour. Budget $60,000–$85,000 fully loaded per machinist-year.

Consider hiring from local community colleges and vocational schools that run machining programs. Fresh graduates need supervision and mentoring but come at $18–$26/hour and can be trained to your shop's specific methods and standards.

Mistakes to Avoid

  • Buying equipment before securing any customers: The most common failure point. Line up at least one customer or subcontract agreement before your first machine arrives.
  • Signing a facility lease without verifying power: CNC machines require 3-phase power. Discovering this after signing a 5-year lease is an expensive problem.
  • Underestimating tooling costs: A new shop needs $10,000–$40,000 in cutting tools, inserts, and holders before making a single part. Tooling is often forgotten in initial budgets.
  • Pricing jobs too low to win business: Undercutting on price to get first customers often leads to cash-flow problems. Know your fully-loaded hourly rate ($75–$150/hour including overhead) before quoting.
  • Neglecting metrology from day one: Quality documentation is required by virtually all commercial customers. A basic CMM and measurement system is not optional — it's the foundation of a credible quality program.
  • Not building working capital reserves: Customers pay Net-30 to Net-60. You pay employees and suppliers weekly. Cash flow management is critical — budget 3–6 months of operating expenses as a reserve.

Equipment Financing for CNC Machine Shops

CNC machine tools finance well because they are productive assets with strong secondary markets. New Haas equipment retains 60–75% of value after 3 years. Financing options:

  • Equipment loans: 36–72 month terms at 6–9% for well-qualified borrowers. Most lenders accept CNC machine tools as collateral with minimal down payment for established businesses.
  • SBA 7(a) loans: Best for startups — SBA guarantee reduces lender risk, enabling financing when conventional lenders say no. Terms up to 10 years for equipment. Requires business plan and personal guarantee.
  • Equipment leasing: Preserves capital, often includes maintenance options. Operating lease payments may be fully deductible. Good for new shops conserving cash.
  • Section 179 deduction: Allows immediate deduction of up to $1,160,000 (2023 limit) of equipment purchases against business income. See our Section 179 guide for details.
  • OEM programs: Haas Financial Services offers competitive rates. Fanuc, Mazak, and DMG MORI also have captive finance programs for new equipment.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does it cost to start a CNC machine shop?

A minimum viable CNC job shop with one used machine costs $72,000–$180,000. A proper two-machine startup with new Haas equipment, full tooling, and working capital runs $218,000–$345,000. A well-equipped 3–5 machine shop ready to pursue aerospace or medical contracts costs $500,000–$800,000 including ISO certification costs. The largest variables are whether you buy new vs. used equipment and how much working capital you hold in reserve.

What licenses do you need to run a CNC machine shop?

No specific machinist license is required in most states. You need a standard business license, OSHA 29 CFR 1910.212 (machine guarding) compliance, and OSHA 29 CFR 1910.147 (lockout/tagout) compliance. ISO 9001 certification is not legally required but is expected by aerospace, medical, and automotive customers. AS9100D (aerospace) and ISO 13485 (medical devices) add market-specific requirements on top of ISO 9001.

How do you find your first CNC machining customers?

Most successful new shops start by subcontracting overflow work from established machine shops — this provides immediate cash flow with no customer acquisition cost. List your capabilities on Thomasnet.com. Register on Xometry and MFG.com where OEMs post RFQs. Drive through local industrial parks and introduce yourself to purchasing managers. Register on SAM.gov for government contracts using NAICS code 332710.

Should you buy new or used CNC machines when starting out?

Used machines reduce upfront capital by 50–60% and preserve cash flow during the critical first year. A used Haas VF-2 runs $25,000–$55,000 vs. $72,000–$90,000 new. The risks: used machines may have hidden wear, limited warranty, and older control software. For customer-facing shops pursuing aerospace or medical certifications, new machines demonstrate commitment and come with OEM warranty. Many successful shops start with used equipment and upgrade as revenue grows.

How long until a CNC machine shop is profitable?

Most new CNC shops reach cash-flow positive within 6–18 months with aggressive customer acquisition. Full profitability on the equipment investment typically takes 2–4 years. The largest variable is utilization rate — machines need to run 6,000–7,000 hours per year (two shifts, 5 days) to generate the revenue needed to service equipment loans, pay labor, and build net profit. One-shift operations typically achieve cash flow positive in year 1 but full ROI in year 3–5.

When do you need ISO 9001 certification for a machine shop?

ISO 9001 certification is not legally required but is expected by most aerospace, medical device, and automotive Tier 1/2 customers. Plan to certify within 1–2 years if targeting these markets. Initial certification costs $10,000–$25,000 including consulting, internal audits, and registrar fees. Annual surveillance audits run $2,000–$5,000. AS9100D (aerospace) and ISO 13485 (medical) build on ISO 9001 and add market-specific requirements — plan for an additional $5,000–$15,000 per additional standard.
