Licensing & Compliance

MSHA Requirements & Mining Permits

Mining operations in the United States are among the most heavily regulated industries. MSHA compliance is a federal requirement — not optional — and lenders financing large mining equipment packages often ask for documentation of compliance status.

  • MSHA Part 46/48 Miner Training — Federal law, required for all mining employees. New miner training: 24 hours minimum. Annual refresher: 8 hours.
  • MSHA 30 CFR Part 56/57 — Safety and health standards for surface (Part 56) and underground (Part 57) metal and nonmetal mines.
  • State Mining Permits — Vary by state and mineral type. Surface mining operations require reclamation bonds in most states.
  • EPA NPDES Permit — Required for water discharge from mining operations (National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System).
  • Blasting License — ATF federal license (FEL) for explosive storage plus state blasting permits required for drill-and-blast operations.
  • Electrical Certifications — Underground mine electrical installations require MSHA-approved designs and certified electricians.
  • Confined Space Entry Training — Required for underground and tank/vessel work under OSHA 1910.146.

For financing eligibility, lenders may require proof of mineral rights, state mine permits, and MSHA operator ID number. See our equipment financing credit requirements guide for full documentation checklists.

Common Questions

Mining Equipment Financing — FAQ

What is the monthly payment on a Cat 793 haul truck?
A new Caterpillar 793F mining haul truck (240-ton payload) costs approximately $3,500,000–$5,000,000. Financed over 84 months at 7% APR, monthly payments run approximately $52,000–$75,000/month. Many large mining haul truck purchases are structured as equipment-backed loans with balloon payments or balloon-to-term structures. equipment lenders offers specialized large mining equipment programs. Most Cat 793 buyers are large mining companies with established credit facilities, so actual rates are often negotiated at the fleet level.
What MSHA certifications are required for mining equipment operators?
Federal MSHA law requires all mining employees to receive miner training under 30 CFR Part 46 (for surface miners at sand, gravel, and stone operations) or Part 48 (for underground and other surface mines). New miner training is 24 hours minimum. Annual refresher training is 8 hours per year. Specific equipment operator certifications (for blasting, for example) are required at the state level. MSHA training records must be maintained and are subject to inspection. Financing lenders for mining equipment typically ask for proof of compliance.
Can I get financing for a small quarry operation?
Yes. Small quarry operations can finance equipment packages including a jaw crusher ($150K–$400K), screen deck ($50K–$150K), and wheel loader ($120K–$300K) through equipment lenders. Typical requirements: 2+ years in business, 660+ personal/business credit, 15–20% down payment, and documentation of aggregate contracts or land/mineral rights. SBA 7(a) loans up to $5M are available for quarry equipment. Many lenders treat aggregate operations favorably because crushed stone is a stable, local-demand commodity.
Is it better to finance new or used mining equipment?
Used mining equipment (3–7 years old) can offer 30–50% savings over new. However, major mining OEMs have certified used programs with inspection reports that make financing easier. Lenders scrutinize used mining equipment carefully — hours, maintenance records, and MSHA compliance documentation are essential. Underground equipment loses value faster due to harsh operating environments. For surface mining, late-model used haul trucks and drills from Tier 1 brands finance at competitive rates. See our new vs. used equipment financing guide for full details.
Can XCMG or Chinese mining equipment be financed in the US?
XCMG, LGMG, and other Chinese mining equipment brands can be financed in the US but require specialty lenders. Standard banks and OEM programs will not finance competing brands. Chinese mining equipment financing typically requires 20–30% down, runs 2–3% higher APR than Tier 1 brands, and may have shorter maximum terms. MSHA compliance documentation must be available regardless of brand. See our American vs. Asian equipment financing guide for a full comparison.
What is the typical down payment for a mining drill rig?
Surface drill rigs ($200K–$2M) typically require 15–20% down for established mining operations. Underground drill rigs may require 0–20% down (0% available for qualified borrowers) due to higher wear in harsh underground environments. Sandvik and Epiroc drills hold their value best and command the most favorable financing terms. New drill rigs from major OEMs may qualify for 10–15% down with strong credit. Blast hole drills over $1M often require detailed mine plan documentation and proven mine life to support the financing. See our credit requirements guide for documentation details.
Are SBA loans available for mining equipment?
Yes. SBA 7(a) loans (up to $5M) and SBA 504 loans (up to $5.5M for equipment and real estate combined) are available for US-based mining and quarry operations. SBA loans offer longer terms (up to 10 years for equipment), lower down payments (10%), and competitive rates. Requirements include US citizen or permanent resident ownership, for-profit business, 2+ years in business, and good personal credit (680+ preferred). Mining operations with federal or state mineral leases must document the lease terms. See our how financing works guide for SBA program details.
How does financing differ between surface and underground mining equipment?
Surface mining equipment (haul trucks, drills, shovels) finances more easily because it has broader resale markets, longer useful life, and easier appraisal. Underground equipment (LHD loaders, jumbo drills, continuous miners) is specialized, experiences faster wear, and has a narrower resale market — making lenders more conservative. Underground equipment typically requires 0–20% down (0% available for qualified borrowers) (vs. 15–20% for surface), shorter maximum terms, and stronger financial statements. Underground longwall systems ($5M–$50M+) are project-financed based on mine reserves and contracts rather than equipment collateral value alone.
