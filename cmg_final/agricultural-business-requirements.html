Federal Registration

USDA Farm Number (FSA Registration)

A USDA Farm Number is the foundational registration for any agricultural operation that intends to participate in federal programs. It is issued by the USDA Farm Service Agency (FSA) and is free to obtain.

Why You Need It

  • Required before applying for any FSA Farm Loan Program (direct or guaranteed)
  • Necessary to enroll in crop insurance programs through USDA Risk Management Agency (RMA)
  • Required for USDA conservation programs including EQIP and CRP
  • Needed for commodity program payments (ARC, PLC)
  • Required for disaster assistance programs (NAP, ELAP, LFP)

How to Register

Visit your local FSA office with a legal description of your farm land or a farm map showing boundaries. Complete Form FSA-156EZ (Streamlined Farm Record). Registration is free and your Farm Number is permanent — it stays with the farm tract, not the operator. If you rent or purchase land that already has a Farm Number, you will update the operator information at the FSA office.

For financing purposes, having an active USDA Farm Number signals to lenders that you are an established agricultural operation with access to federal safety-net programs. See our guide to agricultural equipment financing for more on how this affects loan approval.

Chemical Safety

Pesticide Applicator License

EPA regulates pesticide licensing through FIFRA Section 11, implemented by each state's department of agriculture. Two categories of applicator licenses exist:

Private Applicator

Applies restricted-use pesticides on their own property (or property under their direct control) for agricultural commodity production. Simpler examination. Cost: $25–$60 in most states. Renewal every 3–5 years via continuing education credits or re-examination.

Commercial Applicator

Applies restricted-use pesticides on others' property for compensation, or supervises such application. Requires passing a general standards exam plus one or more category exams:

  • Category 1: Agricultural Pest Control (Field Crops)
  • Category 2: Agricultural Pest Control (Animal)
  • Category 3: Ornamental and Turf
  • Category 5: Industrial, Institutional, Structural, and Health-Related
  • Category 7: Aquatic
  • Category 8: Public Health
  • Category 10: Right-of-Way

Cost: $50–$100 per application plus exam fees. Restricted-Use Pesticides (RUPs) — including many organophosphates, rodenticides, and fumigants — may only be purchased by licensed applicators or directly supervised persons.

Financing tip: pesticide application equipment (spray rigs, airblast sprayers) qualifies for Section 179 deduction in the year of purchase.

Transportation

CDL Requirements for Agricultural Operations

Commercial Driver's License (CDL) requirements apply to agricultural operations when vehicles exceed certain weight thresholds or carry hazardous materials.

When a CDL is Required

  • Class B CDL: Single vehicle with GVWR over 26,001 lb (common grain trucks, bulk liquid tankers)
  • Class A CDL: Combination vehicle where towed unit exceeds 10,001 lb GVWR (semi-trucks pulling grain trailers)
  • Hazmat (H) Endorsement: Required in addition to CDL for anhydrous ammonia transport on public roads

Agricultural Exemptions

Most states provide CDL exemptions for farm operators within 150–200 miles of the home farm for non-commercial, seasonal agricultural operations. Exemptions typically apply to:

  • Farm vehicles transporting farm products, supplies, or machinery
  • Custom harvest operators during harvest season
  • Farm vehicles not used in interstate commerce

Exemptions do NOT apply to: hauling grain for hire, anhydrous ammonia transport, vehicles operated outside the exemption radius, or interstate commerce. Premium Market Access

USDA Organic Certification (NOP)

USDA National Organic Program (NOP) certification is administered through USDA-accredited certifying agents and is required to sell, label, or represent products as "organic." The premium for certified organic can be 20–100% above conventional prices for many crops.

The 3-Year Transition

Before certification, land must have had no prohibited substances (synthetic pesticides, fertilizers, GMOs) applied for 36 months prior to harvest of the first certified organic crop. During transition you cannot market products as organic, which creates a cash flow gap — making equipment financing terms especially important during this period.

Accredited Certifiers (examples)

  • CCOF (California Certified Organic Farmers) — nationwide
  • Oregon Tilth — strong in Pacific Northwest and nationally
  • Midwest Organic Services Association (MOSA) — Midwest focus
  • OneCert — nationwide, competitive pricing
  • OCIA International — multi-country recognition

Annual Costs

Certification costs $400–$1,500/year depending on operation size, certifier, and complexity. Includes annual inspection. USDA EQIP Organic Initiative provides cost-share reimbursement up to $750/year for certification fees. Contact your local NRCS office to enroll. Food Safety

FSMA Produce Safety Rule

The FDA Food Safety Modernization Act (FSMA) Produce Safety Rule establishes science-based minimum standards for the safe growing, harvesting, packing, and holding of fruits and vegetables for human consumption.

Who It Applies To

Farms with $25,000+ in average annual sales of covered produce over the previous 3-year period. "Covered produce" includes most raw agricultural commodities consumed fresh.

Qualified Exemption (Very Small Business)

Farms with $25,000–$250,000 in covered produce sales may qualify for a modified requirements exemption if: total food sales are less than $500,000/year AND the farm primarily sells to direct consumers, restaurants, or retailers in the same state or within 275 miles. Qualified exempt farms must still disclose their name and address on labels or at point of sale.

Core Requirements for Covered Farms

  • Agricultural Water: Testing of irrigation and harvest water; microbial quality criteria for specific uses
  • Worker Hygiene: Training, health and hygiene standards, toilet and handwashing facilities
  • Equipment & Tools: Sanitation procedures, inspection, maintenance
  • Biological Soil Amendments: Requirements for timing and application of compost and manure
  • Wildlife Hazards: Monitoring for wildlife intrusion; response procedures
  • Recordkeeping: 2-year minimum record retention

Irrigation equipment and water testing systems are qualifying equipment for Section 179 deductions — deduct the full cost in the year of purchase.

Hazardous Materials

Anhydrous Ammonia Certification and Compliance

Anhydrous ammonia (NH3) is the most widely used nitrogen fertilizer in the US and also one of the most dangerous common farm chemicals. At -28°F boiling point, it rapidly vaporizes upon release, causing severe chemical burns to eyes, skin, and respiratory system.

State Certification

Most states require specific anhydrous ammonia handler certification beyond a general pesticide license. Requirements typically include a dedicated training course covering safety, emergency response, equipment inspection, and personal protective equipment (full-face respirator, chemical-resistant gloves, water source).

Federal Requirements

  • OSHA 29 CFR 1910.111: Storage and handling of anhydrous ammonia — covers tank design, pressure relief valves, hose specifications, safety shutoffs, and employee training
  • DHS CFATS: Facilities storing more than 10,000 lbs must complete a CSAT Top-Screen at chemicalsecurity.dhs.gov. May be designated as high-risk Chemical Facility under CFATS (Chemical Facility Anti-Terrorism Standards)
  • EPA RMP: Facilities with 10,000+ lbs may also trigger EPA Risk Management Program (RMP) requirements under Clean Air Act Section 112(r)
  • DOT: Transport on public roads requires CDL + Hazmat (H) endorsement; anhydrous ammonia is a hazardous material (UN1005)

Environmental Compliance

Clean Water Act and OSHA Farm Standards

Clean Water Act

NPDES Permits for CAFOs: Concentrated Animal Feeding Operations (CAFOs) above threshold animal unit counts must obtain National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) permits. Large CAFOs (1,000+ animal units) must apply; medium CAFOs may be required based on discharge potential.

Section 401/404: Drainage tile modifications, wetland drainage, and stream alterations may require Army Corps of Engineers Section 404 permits and state Section 401 water quality certifications. Agricultural exemptions under Section 404(f) exist for normal farming activities on established agricultural land but do not cover conversion of wetlands.

OSHA 29 CFR 1928 — Agricultural Industry Standards

  • 1928.51: Rollover Protective Structures (ROPS) required on tractors manufactured after October 25, 1976
  • 1928.57: Guarding of farm field equipment, farmstead equipment, and cotton gins
  • 1928.110: Field sanitation — toilet facilities, potable water, hand-washing for farms with 11+ hand-labor employees
  • SMV emblems: Required on slow-moving equipment (under 25 mph) operated on public roads

Note: OSHA's agricultural standards apply differently based on farm size. Farms with fewer than 11 employees that do not use temporary labor contractors are exempt from OSHA inspection under the annual congressional appropriations rider — but OSHA standards still represent best practice and state OSHA plans may differ.

ROPS-equipped tractors and properly guarded equipment are essential for farm operation financing. Lenders financing tractors or John Deere equipment expect safety compliance.

Common Questions

Agricultural Licensing — FAQ

How do I get a USDA Farm Number?
Visit your local USDA Farm Service Agency (FSA) office and complete Form FSA-156EZ. Registration is free and required before applying for any USDA programs including crop insurance, FSA loans, and conservation programs. You will need to provide a legal description of your land or a farm map. The Farm Number is linked to your farm tract and is permanent.
What are the pesticide applicator license requirements?
Every state has its own pesticide applicator licensing program administered under EPA's FIFRA Section 11. Private applicators (applying on their own property) take a simpler exam. Commercial applicators (applying on others' property) must pass category-specific exams. Exam costs range $25–$100. Restricted-Use Pesticides can only be purchased by licensed applicators or under their direct supervision.
Are there CDL exemptions for farmers?
Yes. Most states provide agricultural exemptions from CDL requirements for farm vehicles operated within 150–200 miles of the home farm. However, the exemption typically applies only to non-commercial farm operations. Grain hauling for hire, anhydrous ammonia transport, and vehicles over 26,001 lb GVWR operated outside the exemption radius generally require a full CDL with appropriate endorsements.
How much does organic certification cost and how long does it take?
USDA Organic certification costs $400–$1,500 per year depending on operation size and certifier. Before you can certify, you must complete a mandatory 3-year transition period during which no prohibited substances can be applied. USDA EQIP programs offer cost-share reimbursement up to $750/year for organic transition costs. After initial certification, annual inspection and renewal fees apply.
Does the FSMA Produce Safety Rule apply to my farm?
The FSMA Produce Safety Rule applies to farms with $25,000 or more in average annual sales of covered produce. A Very Small Business exemption is available for farms with $25,000–$250,000 in covered produce sales if total food sales are under $500,000 and they sell primarily to direct consumers, restaurants, or retailers in the same state or within 275 miles. Requirements include agricultural water testing, worker hygiene, equipment sanitation, and wildlife hazard management.
What license do I need to handle anhydrous ammonia?
Handling anhydrous ammonia requires state-specific certification in most states. Storage and handling must comply with OSHA 29 CFR 1910.111. Operations storing more than 10,000 lbs must complete a DHS CSAT Top-Screen. Transporting anhydrous ammonia on public roads requires a CDL with Hazmat (H) endorsement.
What do I need for an FSA Farm Loan?
FSA Farm Loans require a USDA Farm Number, proof of farming experience or training, a farm business plan, evidence that you were unable to obtain credit elsewhere (for direct loans), acceptable credit history, and sufficient collateral. FSA Direct Farm Operating Loans go up to $600,000; Ownership Loans up to $1.5 million. Guaranteed loans through commercial lenders backed by FSA have higher limits. Explore how farm income affects your loan qualification.
Can I get EQIP cost-share for organic certification?
Yes. USDA's Environmental Quality Incentives Program (EQIP) Organic Initiative reimburses up to $750 per year for organic certification costs and provides additional payments for implementing required organic practices. Certified organic producers receive higher payment rates for conservation practices than conventional producers. Contact your local NRCS office to apply. This can meaningfully offset the 3-year transition period's financial burden.
