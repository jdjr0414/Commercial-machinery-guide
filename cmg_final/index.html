Equipment Financing Process

How Commercial Equipment Financing Works

Equipment financing uses the equipment itself as collateral, making it more accessible than unsecured business loans. Here's the typical process.

1️⃣

Choose Your Equipment

Identify the equipment you need — new or used, specific model or general category. Get a quote or invoice from the dealer or seller. This determines your financing amount.

2️⃣

Apply for Financing

Submit a financing application with basic business information, bank statements, and the equipment invoice. Many lenders can approve in 24-48 hours for straightforward equipment deals.

3️⃣

Get Approved

Lender reviews your credit, business revenue, and the equipment's collateral value. Equipment with strong resale value often earns better rates. Approval amounts are typically 80-100% of equipment value.

4️⃣

Acquire Your Equipment

Lender pays the seller directly. You take delivery of the equipment and begin making monthly payments. The equipment generates revenue that services the loan. At payoff, you own it free and clear.

Licensing & Certification

What Licenses Do You Need?

Operating commercial equipment often requires specific licenses, certifications, and insurance. Here's what different industries require.

🌲 Tree Service

  • ISA Certified Arborist (recommended)
  • State contractor's license (varies by state)
  • General liability insurance ($1M+ minimum)
  • Workers' compensation insurance
  • Chipper/equipment operator training
  • OSHA 10/30 certification recommended

🏗️ Construction / Excavating

  • State contractor's license (most states require)
  • Heavy equipment operator certification
  • OSHA 10/30 required on many job sites
  • General liability insurance ($1M-$5M)
  • Commercial auto for equipment transport
  • Environmental compliance certifications

🏭 Manufacturing / CNC

  • Business license (state and local)
  • OSHA machine guarding compliance
  • Quality management (ISO 9001 for contracts)
  • Fire safety and sprinkler requirements
  • Environmental permits if applicable
  • Electrical permits for installation

🌾 Agriculture

  • Pesticide applicator license (if spraying)
  • Commercial driver's license (CDL) for large loads
  • USDA farm registration
  • Organic certification (if applicable)
  • Food safety certifications (GAP, GFSI)
  • State ag department registrations

Common Questions

Equipment Financing FAQ

What is commercial equipment financing?
Commercial equipment financing is a loan or lease used to purchase business equipment. The equipment typically serves as collateral, which means you can often get approved with lower credit scores than unsecured business loans. Payments are spread over the useful life of the equipment — typically 2-7 years — while the equipment generates revenue to service the debt. At the end of the loan term, you own the equipment outright.
What credit score do I need to finance equipment?
Traditional bank lenders typically require 650+ credit scores. Equipment-specific lenders and alternative financing companies often work with scores as low as 500-550, especially when the equipment has strong collateral value. The condition, age, and resale value of the equipment matters as much as your credit. A newer Caterpillar excavator is much easier to finance than a 20-year-old specialty machine.
Should I finance new or used equipment?
Both have advantages. New equipment comes with manufacturer warranties, the latest technology, and typically better financing rates. Used equipment costs significantly less upfront and can be financed too, though rates may be slightly higher and loan terms shorter. For equipment that depreciates quickly (like technology), buying used often makes sense. For heavy iron that holds value (like Caterpillar or John Deere equipment), new financing with a long warranty is often worth the premium.
What is the difference between an equipment loan and a lease?
With an equipment loan, you own the equipment from day one and build equity in it. Monthly payments may be slightly higher, but you own a depreciating asset that can be sold or used as collateral. With a lease, you use the equipment for a set term and may have an option to purchase at the end. Lease payments are often lower, and you can upgrade to newer equipment more easily. Loans make more sense for long-lasting equipment; leases work better for technology that becomes obsolete quickly.
Can new businesses or startups finance equipment?
Yes, though it's harder than for established businesses. Most traditional lenders want 1-2 years of business history. However, equipment financing companies that specialize in startup situations exist. They typically require stronger personal credit (680+), a larger down payment (20-30%), and may require additional collateral or a personal guarantee. The equipment's value as collateral often compensates for the lack of business history.
What is Section 179 and how does it apply to equipment financing?
Section 179 of the IRS tax code allows businesses to deduct the full purchase price of qualifying equipment in the year it's purchased, rather than depreciating it over several years. For 2024, the deduction limit is $1.22 million. This means you can finance equipment, receive the tax deduction upfront, and use the tax savings to help make payments. Many equipment purchases qualify, making financing even more attractive on an after-tax basis. Consult your tax advisor for specifics.
How long does equipment financing approval take?
Simple equipment deals under $150,000 can often be approved in 24-48 hours with basic documentation. Larger transactions, used equipment, or complex credit situations take longer — typically 3-7 business days. Having your last 3-6 months of bank statements, a copy of your driver's license, and an equipment invoice or quote ready speeds the process significantly.
How much down payment is required for equipment financing?
Many equipment financing programs offer 0% down — 100% financing — for qualified borrowers with strong credit and established businesses. More commonly, lenders ask for 10-20% down. The down payment requirement depends on your credit score, time in business, the equipment's collateral value, and the lender's program. Startups or lower credit situations typically require 20-30% down.
