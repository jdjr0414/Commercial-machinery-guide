Company Background

About Gradall Industries — An American Original

Gradall Industries, Inc. is headquartered and manufactures all of its equipment in New Philadelphia, Ohio. The company traces its roots to the 1940s when the first Gradall telescopic boom excavator was developed for highway maintenance work — a task that conventional excavators of the era could not perform effectively. Today, Gradall remains the dominant manufacturer of telescopic boom excavators in the US market.

Gradall is owned by The Gradall Company and employs several hundred workers in New Philadelphia. The company manufactures the complete XL series of wheeled telescopic excavators along with the D262 crawler model. Being American-made gives Gradall a meaningful advantage for government procurement, including Buy American requirements on federally-funded highway projects.

The Gradall customer base is highly concentrated — most buyers are state DOTs, utility companies, municipalities, and specialty contractors who know exactly what the machine does and why they need it. This is not a machine buyers stumble upon; it is almost always a deliberate, specification-driven purchase.

What Makes Gradall Different

The Telescopic Boom Advantage

Understanding why buyers choose Gradall over conventional excavators is essential for financing context. The telescopic boom provides capabilities that simply cannot be replicated with a standard excavator:

  • Highway median work: The boom extends over jersey barriers and median walls to mow grass, clear debris, and maintain slopes without entering live traffic lanes. State DOTs use this capability daily on highway fleets.
  • Bridge inspection and repair: The boom can reach under bridge decks, work at extreme angles, and position precisely in locations where no other excavator can safely operate. Bridge maintenance contractors specify Gradall for this reason.
  • Slope grading: The boom tilts to work at angles parallel to slope faces — critical for maintaining embankments, ditch lines, and drainage channels along highways. Conventional excavators cannot replicate this angular precision.
  • Pipeline and utility work: Precision digging in tight utility corridors, working alongside buried infrastructure with millimeter-level control that reduces the risk of utility strikes.

This concentrated application profile explains why Gradall's buyer base is narrow but loyal. Most Gradall buyers are replacing a previous Gradall — they already know the machine's value. Private contractors financing Gradall are almost always pursuing specific work where the machine is the decisive competitive advantage.

Financing Pathways

How Gradall Financing Works — Private vs Government Buyers

Private Contractor Financing

Private contractors financing a Gradall XL need specialty equipment lenders. Major banks are less familiar with Gradall than Caterpillar, but the machines have a loyal buyer base and decent residual values — lenders with experience in construction equipment can be comfortable with Gradall as collateral. Typical terms for private buyers:

  • Down payment: 15–20% for established contractors with strong credit (640+)
  • Interest rate: 7–9.5% APR depending on credit, time in business, and lender
  • Loan term: 48–72 months
  • Best lenders: DLL, Beacon Funding, Channel Partners Capital, specialty construction equipment lenders
  • Key application requirement: Evidence of demand for Gradall-specific work (DOT subcontracts, utility company relationships, bridge work history)

Government and DOT Financing

State DOTs, municipalities, and utility companies have entirely different financing pathways and generally better terms than private buyers:

  • Tax-Exempt Lease-Purchase (TELP): Municipal entities can finance equipment with tax-exempt rates as low as 3–5% over 5–10 years. This is by far the most common government financing mechanism for Gradall equipment.
  • Cooperative purchasing contracts: Sourcewell, NASPO ValuePoint, and state cooperative agreements allow direct purchase of Gradall equipment without individual competitive bid processes, streamlining procurement significantly.
  • State highway improvement funds: Many state DOTs purchase highway maintenance equipment through dedicated capital equipment funds with multi-year appropriations.
  • FHWA funding: Gradall's American-made status (New Philadelphia, Ohio) satisfies Buy American provisions for federally-funded highway maintenance projects.

Common Questions

Gradall Equipment Financing — FAQ

What is a telescopic excavator and why is Gradall unique?
A telescopic excavator uses a boom that extends and retracts telescopically, unlike a conventional excavator's articulated (jointed) boom. This allows the Gradall XL series to work at a much wider range of reach distances and angles from a single setup position. The boom can tilt to work on slopes, reach under bridge decks, extend over highway barriers, or precisely excavate in confined utility corridors. No conventional excavator can replicate this range of motion. Gradall Industries (New Philadelphia, Ohio) has manufactured this unique design since the 1940s and remains the dominant US manufacturer of telescopic boom excavators.
Gradall vs Cat wheeled excavator — which should I buy?
A Gradall XL and a Cat M-series wheeled excavator are both rubber-tired road-mobile machines, but they serve fundamentally different applications. A Cat M315 or M322 is a general-purpose excavator on rubber tires for urban trench work and general excavation. A Gradall XL 4100 V is a specialized precision tool for highway mowing, bridge work, slope grading, and reach-over-barrier applications. If you need a versatile urban excavator, buy the Cat wheeled. If you do highway maintenance, bridge repair, or slope work, the Gradall's telescopic boom is irreplaceable. See our excavator financing guide and Caterpillar equipment financing guide for Cat comparisons.
Who uses Gradall equipment and who buys it?
The primary Gradall buyers are: (1) State DOTs — highway maintenance fleets for mowing, guardrail work, sign installation, and slope maintenance; (2) Utility companies — electric, gas, and water utilities for precise excavation along rights-of-way; (3) Municipal public works departments; (4) Specialty contractors doing bridge repair, slope stabilization, and pipeline work. Many state DOTs specify Gradall by name in their equipment procurement specs because no other machine matches its capabilities for roadside work.
How do I finance a Gradall as a small contractor?
Financing a Gradall as a small private contractor requires specialty equipment lenders. Major banks are less familiar with Gradall than Caterpillar, but the machines hold value well and have a loyal buyer base. Typical requirements: 15–20% down payment, 640+ personal credit for established contractors, and evidence of work pipeline showing demand for Gradall-specific work. The most effective approach is specialty construction equipment lenders (DLL, Beacon Funding, Channel Partners Capital) alongside any dealer financing Gradall offers. See our guide on financing specialty and less-known equipment brands for broader strategies.
How do government agencies and DOTs purchase Gradall equipment?
State DOTs and government agencies typically acquire Gradall equipment through: (1) Competitive bid procurement based on performance specifications; (2) Tax-Exempt Lease-Purchase (TELP) agreements at 3–5% interest over 5–10 years; (3) Cooperative purchasing contracts (Sourcewell, NASPO ValuePoint) that allow direct purchase without individual competitive bidding. Gradall's American-made status in New Philadelphia, Ohio satisfies Buy American provisions for federally-funded highway maintenance projects — a meaningful competitive advantage.
What are the maintenance costs for Gradall telescopic excavators?
Gradall XL series machines have higher maintenance costs than comparable conventional excavators due to the telescopic boom system — boom seals, telescoping cylinders, and guidance components require regular inspection and periodic replacement. A well-maintained Gradall XL 4100 V can reach 15,000–20,000 hours with major component overhauls. Estimated annual maintenance for approximately 1,000–1,500 hours/year use: $8,000–$18,000 depending on age and utilization. Major repairs require qualified Gradall technicians. Factor dealer-supported service into your operating cost model when evaluating ownership.
What is the used Gradall equipment market like?
The used Gradall market is specialized but active. Machines appear regularly on Ritchie Bros., IronPlanet, and through Gradall dealers. Gradall XL series machines typically retain 50–65% of original value at 3 years for well-maintained examples, and 35–50% at 5 years — respectable residuals for a specialty machine with a loyal buyer base. DOT fleet machines with documented maintenance histories command premium pricing. Financing used Gradall follows the same specialty lender path as new. See our American vs Asian equipment financing guide for context on American-made specialty equipment values.
Gradall vs JCB wheeled excavator — what is the difference?
JCB makes wheeled excavators (JS145W, JS220W) popular in Europe and growing in the US. Like Cat M-series, JCB wheeled excavators use a conventional articulated boom and are general-purpose road-mobile machines. They do not have a telescopic boom. Gradall's XL series is not competing for the same applications. If you need general trench work on city streets, the JCB JS145W or Cat M322 are viable options. If you need highway shoulder work, bridge repair, or slope grading requiring telescopic reach, only Gradall offers this capability in the US market at this scale.
